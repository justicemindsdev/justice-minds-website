Case Study: International Graduate Employment

"Sick—Can't Leave My Brother." But He Still Makes the 11pm Shift

Inside the face calculus keeping Britain's international graduates stuck at £12 an hour

By Ben Mak • 8 min read

Shubham's text lands at 18:53: "Brother is sick right now, can't leave him."
At 23:00 he clocks in for his £12-per-hour night shift.

In between is a different invitation: a recorded, ten-minute demo that would unlock a two-week executive coaching practicum under a future judge and Parliamentary-acknowledged expert—Ben Mak, whose consultancy time is billed at £750 per hour to corporate clients. The goal? Not another certificate to frame. Executive-level employability—the kind that moves you from warehouse shifts to professional roles with £35,000–£50,000 starting salaries.

Shubham postpones the demo to "tomorrow." The night shift gets the yes.

On paper this makes no sense. In practice it's coherent. He protects the obligation that is familiar, legible, and low risk to face—and delays the one that is high exposure, recorded, and therefore high risk to face.

The mathematics of what's actually on offer

Let's be precise about what Shubham is declining:

  • 20+ hours of 1-to-1 executive coaching from a judicial-level forensic investigator whose rate card reads £750/hour. Commercial value: £15,000+
  • Portfolio development with authenticated, employer-ready evidence packs—the kind that bypass HR filters and land you in front of hiring managers. Market value for portfolio coaching: £5,000–£8,000
  • Recorded demos with feedback rubrics—time-stamped proof of competence in Decision-Making, Stakeholder Management, Communication, and Method. These aren't module grades; they're portable proof you can link in applications
  • Live practicum experience under NDA and company liability, producing real micro-artifacts for real workflows. Not an internship—an audition with evidence

Total estimated value if purchased commercially: £20,000–£25,000.

Cost to Shubham: Zero. The investment is entirely Ben's.

What the £12/hour shift offers in return

Let's be equally precise about what the night shift provides:

  • Wage: £12/hour for an 8-hour shift = £96 per night, minus tax and deductions = ~£82 take-home
  • Skills development: None. The role is process-driven, repetitive, and designed to be replaceable
  • Career trajectory: Horizontal. You can do this job for five years and your CV won't change
  • Employer investment in you: Zero. If you quit tonight, they'll fill your spot by tomorrow. You are a shift code, not a name
  • Evidence for future employers: "Warehouse operative, 2023–2025." No portfolio, no demos, no proof of executive competence

The employer doesn't know Shubham's brother is sick. They don't care. If he no-shows, they dock his pay and move on. The job doesn't love him back.

Meanwhile, Ben keeps showing up

Here's what makes this situation both remarkable and tragic:

Ben has already invested over 20 hours of executive coaching time with Shubham. At his commercial rate, that's £15,000 of uncompensated consultancy. Not as charity—Ben doesn't do pity—but as an investment in potential he can see and Shubham can't.

Ben has:

  • Reviewed Shubham's background and identified transferable enterprise-level skills Shubham was describing as "just helping my uncle"
  • Designed a bespoke two-week practicum structure with rubric-based scoring—no subjective "you're not good enough," just measurable gaps and clear next steps
  • Offered to personally coach him through recorded demos, providing frame-by-frame feedback on body language, pacing, and stakeholder framing
  • Structured everything to be face-safe: private first drafts, NDA-protected work, no "gotcha" moments—just timed evidence and professional correction
  • Made the entry requirement absurdly simple: one ten-minute demo. Not a 5,000-word essay. Not an exam. Just show up on camera for ten minutes and talk through a micro-task

And Shubham says "tomorrow" for fourteen days straight. Then clocks in for the £12 shift that will forget his name the second he quits.

The loop in plain sight

Across a fortnight the messages repeat: studying… practicing… tomorrow. No time, no file, no demo. Yet the £12 shift is never in doubt. The contrast doesn't reveal laziness; it reveals a learned survival strategy:

  • Certain wage > uncertain, recorded proof. The shift pays now and can't be replayed. The demo is a timestamped artifact that can be replayed—and judged.
  • Private effort > public evidence. Tutorials feel productive without the danger of being seen. A demo is public and permanent.
  • Duty to family > duty to portfolio. "Brother is sick" reads as care. "I have a ten-minute demo" reads as self-promotion.

Indian psychology: face and reprimand

For many Indian families, izzat (face) is communal currency. Errors aren't purely personal—they echo across kin, caste, and community. Better to be unseen than to be seen stumbling. Add a second dynamic: reprimand at home is often public and top-down; affection is private. British executive culture reverses this: praise is public; reprimand is procedural, hidden in process. So Western kindness can feel suspect; Western accountability can feel like rejection.

Translate those logics into a graduate's day and Shubham's choices map cleanly:

  • A recorded demo equals the risk of public loss-of-face.
  • A shift equals reliability—visible duty without exposure.
  • "Tomorrow" buys time to avoid both shame and confrontation.

What the system rewards

Universities extract international fees; recruiters demand "evidence of impact" while outsourcing training risk. Families underwrite degrees, not the authenticated proof that moves hiring committees. Graduates absorb the cost of delay. The rational play—if your operating system is face-safety—is to defend the predictable £12 obligation and postpone the ten-minute audition that could change your life.

The practicum Shubham keeps postponing

The offer on the table is modest by design: ship one micro-artifact (however small), then a 10-minute recorded demo under company liability and NDAs; repeat twice in two weeks. Everything is time-stamped, logged, and scored by rubric, producing an employer-ready evidence pack. Not a bypass of applications—an authenticated entry.

Here's what successful completion unlocks:

  • A portfolio of verified work with time-stamps, rubric scores, and evidence links. Not "I can do stakeholder management"—proof you did stakeholder management, scored 3.5/4, here's the recording
  • Professional references from a judicial-level expert whose LinkedIn endorsement opens doors Shubham's degree never will
  • Executive communication skills trained through live feedback—how to frame complex problems, manage up, and deliver under time pressure without collapsing into apology
  • A pathway out of £12/hour roles into graduate management schemes, consultancy entry-points, or corporate analyst positions where starting salaries are £35,000–£50,000

The two-week commitment? Roughly 20 hours total—less than two night shifts. But those 20 hours could 3x his annual income within six months.

Shubham agrees to the structure. He even requests Ben's rate card to show his family the value. He asks about timing, logistics, NDAs—all the questions that signal genuine interest.

Then the night shift wins again. And again. And again.

The employer who doesn't know his brother is sick

Here's the detail that should break any rational decision-making model:

The £12/hour warehouse doesn't know Shubham's brother is sick. They don't know his name. They don't know he has a Master's degree. They don't know he managed supply-chain logistics for his uncle's transport business back home. They don't care.

If Shubham texts them at 18:53 saying "Brother is sick, can't make the shift," they'll text back: "We need someone tonight. Can you come or not?" If he says no, they'll call the next person on the list. No grace. No flexibility. No relationship.

But Ben? Ben has spent 20+ hours understanding Shubham's background, his cognitive patterns, his family dynamics, and his hidden competencies. Ben knows Shubham helped coordinate cross-border logistics for his uncle's lorry business—work that involved customs paperwork, route optimization, and real-time problem-solving when trucks broke down in Punjab at 2am.

Ben knows this because he asked. And listened. And then said: "That's not 'helping uncle.' That's supply-chain operations management. Let me show you how to translate it."

So when Shubham says "Brother is sick," Ben doesn't dock his pay. Ben says: "I understand. Family first. But the demo is ten minutes. We can do it tomorrow morning, tomorrow evening, or Sunday afternoon. Which works?"

And Shubham says "tomorrow."

The warehouse that will replace him in 24 hours gets his time. The mentor who has invested £15,000+ in coaching gets "tomorrow."

What reasonable minds could conclude

  • He isn't avoiding work; he's avoiding visible risk.
  • The market overvalues degrees and undervalues portable proof.
  • Without a face-safe on-ramp to being seen, bright graduates will keep choosing reliability over leverage.

Designing for face—without coddling

Face-Safe Design Principles

  • Private first exposure, public later. Start with a ten-minute demo in a closed room; circulate the clip after. The first "loss" is survivable; the first "win" is replayable.
  • Reprimand as rubric, not tone. Score Decision, Method, Communication, Stakeholders on a 4-point scale. Circle, correct, move on. No speeches. Correction becomes process, not humiliation.
  • Face-neutral deadlines. "No artifact, no meeting" is cleaner than "you failed." Two misses release the seat. Consequence without shame theatre.
  • Translate status. "Farm" becomes agricultural production. "Uncle's lorry" becomes supply-chain logistics. Identity moves from entry-level to enterprise without boast.
  • Parents as investors. Fund evidence, not more modules: demos, artifacts, verification links. Protect family face by buying proof, not volume.

Why this pattern is so hard to break

Here's the psychological trap Shubham is caught in—and it's not stupidity, it's survival logic operating in the wrong context:

The warehouse shift is low-stakes humiliation. No one watches. No one scores. If you mess up, you fix it, and the conveyor belt keeps moving. The shame is private and temporary.

The recorded demo is high-stakes visibility. Someone will watch. Someone will score. If you mess up, it's timestamped and permanent. The shame could be public and replayable.

So the brain does this calculation: "I can survive another £12 shift. I might not survive a recorded failure that Ben can replay, or that my family could one day see."

Except Ben isn't recording to shame him. Ben is recording to validate him—to give him portable proof that says "Here's what I can do" instead of "Here's what my degree says I studied."

But Shubham's operating system doesn't have that framework yet. He's been trained—by family, by exam culture, by visa precarity—to see recording as risk, not evidence. To see feedback as humiliation, not calibration.

What we're actually trying to do here

This isn't a pity piece. Shubham doesn't need sympathy; he needs a pattern interrupt.

Ben's goal—the reason he's invested £15,000+ of time—is to show Shubham that the math has changed:

  • The demo is safer than the shift because it's done under NDA, scored by rubric, and framed as learning—not judgment
  • The portfolio is more valuable than the wage because it opens doors the degree couldn't and the shift never will
  • The "failure" he's afraid of doesn't exist in this system—there's only "scored 2.5/4, here's how to get to 3.5, try again tomorrow"

But explaining this doesn't work. Shubham agrees with the logic. He says "yes, I understand, I'll do it tomorrow."

Then 11pm rolls around and the shift wins.

The question that decides everything

When Shubham says "can't leave my brother," the system nods. When he says "I'll do the demo tomorrow," the system shrugs. The £12 shift protects present face; the recorded proof would threaten it now to multiply it later.

So the only honest question left—the one employers, families and graduates must answer—is this:

"Which obligation do we want a graduate to protect first—the employer who doesn't know his name, or the mentor who's invested £15,000 learning who he could become?"

Until the answer flips, "tomorrow" will keep winning. And Britain will keep training its capable invisibles to stay exactly that: capable, and invisible.

The irony that should wake someone up

Here's the final piece that makes this whole situation almost unbearable to watch:

Shubham works night shifts at a warehouse where, if he called in sick, they'd replace him by morning and never notice he was gone.

But he won't call in sick. He protects that obligation like it's sacred.

Meanwhile, Ben—who has spent 20+ hours coaching him, who has designed a bespoke practicum, who has offered him a pathway to triple his income—Ben gets "tomorrow."

The institution that sees him as a shift code gets his loyalty. The person who sees him as a future executive gets excuses.

If that doesn't reveal the depth of the face-safety trap, nothing will.

So what happens next?

One of three things:

Option 1: Shubham does the demo. Ten minutes. One micro-task. Recorded, scored, corrected. He survives it. Realizes the sky didn't fall. Does it again. Two weeks later he has a portfolio, a reference, and a pathway out.

Option 2: Shubham keeps saying "tomorrow." The seat gets released. Someone else takes it. Shubham stays at £12/hour, wondering why his Master's degree didn't change anything, never quite connecting the dots that the opportunity was there and he chose the shift.

Option 3: Someone who loves Shubham reads this and says: "This is you. This is exactly you. And you need to stop."

Because sometimes the only way to break a pattern is to see it written down—in detail, with math, with no room for "tomorrow."

Ben has done his part. He's invested the time, designed the system, removed every barrier except one: Shubham has to show up.

The question isn't whether he's capable. Ben already knows he is. The question is whether Shubham can choose the ten-minute risk over the eight-hour certainty.

If you're reading this and you know someone stuck in this exact loop—someone brilliant, someone capable, someone who keeps choosing the shift over the demo—send them this article.

And if you're reading this and it's you—Shubham, or someone like him—then hear this:

"The warehouse will forget you the day you quit. But the portfolio you're too afraid to build? That follows you forever. One of these protects you for tonight. The other protects you for life. Choose."

Shubham: This is Not to Shame—It's to Shine

Let me be crystal clear about what this article is—and what it isn't.

This is not to shame you. This is to shine a light on what you're capable of.

With my coaching, you will not be brought to shame. There is no humiliation in my practicum—only measurement, correction, and elevation. The only shame that exists here is self-made: the shame of not trying when the door was wide open.

Your culture taught you that face must be saved if you're seen even attempting something difficult. They conditioned you to believe that only the perception of the attained goal matters—and to erase the blood, sweat, and tears it took to get there. That's why you hide your effort. That's why you postpone the demo.

But here's what you need to understand:

"Persistence isn't problematic. Pretending is."

I don't score you on whether you're already perfect. I score you on Decision, Method, Communication, and Stakeholder Management—and then I show you exactly where you're at and exactly what to fix. No drama. No speeches. No shame theatre.

Just rubric, correction, and growth.

Together, we will get you to your highest potential—not only because you deserve it, but because you were designed to do great things.

Don't trust me. Trust the evidence:

  • You coordinated cross-border logistics for your uncle's transport business—work that involved customs, route optimization, and real-time crisis management
  • You completed a Master's degree in a foreign country while navigating visa systems, cultural adaptation, and financial precarity
  • You show up for £12/hour shifts with military reliability—even when your brother is sick

You are capable. You are reliable. You do have enterprise-level skills.

What you don't have—yet—is the authenticated proof that translates those skills into a language employers recognize and value.

That's what the practicum gives you. That's what the portfolio unlocks. That's what the ten-minute demo starts.

The door is still open. The seat is still available. But "tomorrow" has a deadline.

You can keep protecting the shift that will replace you by morning. Or you can take the ten-minute risk that could change your life.

The choice is yours. But choose now—because "tomorrow" just ran out.
