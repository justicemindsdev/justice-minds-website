Measuring What Matters: Why Exams Fail and Conversation Succeeds

A forensic investigator's approach to measuring competence through continuous observation, linguistic patterns, and applied performance—not artificial time pressure

By Ben Mak • 12 min read

I was 31 years old when I realized I wasn't stupid. Three decades of believing the education system's verdict on my intelligence—because I couldn't perform in exam conditions. I'd freeze under time pressure, second-guess obvious answers, and walk out knowing I understood the material but couldn't prove it when it mattered.

Then I became a forensic investigator. And I learned something that changed everything: The way we measure competence is fundamentally broken.

Exams don't measure intelligence. They measure performance under artificial time pressure. That's not the same thing. Not even close.

The Forensic Alternative: Continuous Observation

In forensic investigation, we don't rely on snapshots. We track patterns over time. We look for consistency, for micro-indicators that reveal true understanding versus surface-level mimicry. We measure what people do, not what they can regurgitate under stress.

Dr. Carol Dweck's research on growth mindset reveals that traditional testing creates what she calls "performance anxiety environments"—where the focus shifts from learning to proving, from understanding to performing.[1]"When students are evaluated through high-stakes testing, their cognitive resources shift from deep processing to anxiety management. The measurement tool fundamentally alters what's being measured."

Over 1,800 hours of intensive mentoring work, I've developed forensic assessment methods that reveal competence traditional exams miss entirely. Let me show you what I track—and why it matters more than any test score.

Linguistic Forensics: What Word Choice Reveals

Every conversation leaves evidence. As a forensic investigator, I don't just hear words—I analyze patterns that reveal cognitive structures the speaker doesn't consciously control.

The "I" Statement Test

Watch how someone describes challenges:

  • "I need to improve my time management" — Takes ownership, internal locus of control
  • "They don't give us enough time" — External blame, learned helplessness
  • "People say I'm disorganized" — Deflection, identity through others' perception

The shift from "they/people" language to "I" language marks a measurable transformation. It indicates someone moving from passive recipient to active agent. That's not something you can fake in a two-hour exam.

Psychologist Dr. James Pennebaker's research on linguistic markers shows that pronoun usage patterns predict psychological health, leadership capacity, and professional success with remarkable accuracy.[2]"The use of first-person singular pronouns correlates with personal accountability and emotional intelligence. Individuals who consistently use 'I' statements demonstrate 73% higher problem-solving efficacy in complex situations."

The Sentence Completion Indicator

Here's one of my most reliable forensic markers: Can someone accurately finish your sentences?

Not interrupting. Not guessing. Actually demonstrating structural understanding deep enough to predict logical conclusions. When this happens consistently across complex topics, you're witnessing internalized competence.

I track this across 286 recorded sessions with multiple mentees. The moment someone stops waiting for instructions and starts anticipating needs, when they catch their own logical inconsistencies mid-sentence and self-correct—that's neural reorganization happening in real time.
